01 The ask

The proposal, in one paragraph

I am asking for one thing, and it is smaller than it may first look: an eighteen-month, fixed-term sponsorship onto the Level 4 AI and Automation Practitioner apprenticeship, so I can do real work inside the AI capability you are building while the government funds the training.

To be completely clear about what this is not. I am not asking for a permanent job, and I am not asking you to commit to me indefinitely. This is a defined, temporary arrangement with an end date already built in: fifteen months of the programme, the twelve-week end-point assessment, and then it stops unless you choose otherwise.

At the end of the term, the call is entirely yours. If eighteen months of real work has earned a permanent place, I would be glad to stay. If it has not, the arrangement simply ends, we part on good terms, and I move on. You hold that exit the whole way through, and I would far rather set it out plainly than leave you wondering about it.

In between, here is the exchange. You get a builder on your real problems from month one, at apprentice terms, with the training already paid for and the exact gap your feedback named being closed month by month. I get the structured depth I was told to go and build, at the one company I have wanted to join throughout all of this.

That is the whole ask: eighteen months, funded, and your decision at the end.

02 Why now

Why now, and why this fits

PortSwigger is early in a serious internal AI transformation, and hiring a new kind of team to drive it. The AI Pioneer brief describes the job precisely: embed with a team, find where the friction really is, build something that works, evaluate it honestly, hand it over, move on.

Separately, the UK government designed an apprenticeship standard for exactly this moment. The Level 4 AI and Automation Practitioner programme exists so employers can build internal AI and automation capability by training someone on their real work, inside the business, with the training bill covered.

Put the two side by side and the overlap is hard to ignore.

03 Candour

Closing the gap you named

I applied for AI Pioneer and reached the later stages of the process. Your feedback was direct, and I am glad it was: the strongest candidates were already building multi-agent autonomous workflows, their own APIs and MCPs as day-to-day work. I was not at that depth yet.

I am not going to argue with the feedback. Instead I went looking for the fastest structured way to close it.

Phase 2 of this programme is built around exactly that gap

Unit 6 covers API integration in depth: authentication, authorisation, data contracts, resilience and failure handling. Unit 7 covers agentic AI: agent design, prompt orchestration, human-in-the-loop patterns, guardrails and validation. Unit 8 turns assurance into habit: testing, monitoring, release packs and runbooks.

The syllabus is the floor, not the ceiling. Alongside the course I will keep building my own projects at the deeper end, including MCP servers and multi-agent systems, because self-directed building is how everything in the section above got made.

Stated just as plainly

I am neurodivergent: ADHD and dyslexia. It is a large part of why I spot patterns and think in systems quickly, and why I cannot leave a problem alone once I start. It also means structure brings out my best. The programme's monthly coaching and built-in neurodiversity support are exactly the scaffolding that turns that intensity into consistent, clearly communicated work. I would rather tell you this straight than have you wonder.

08 The funding

How the funding works

The training itself costs £18,000, met through the UK apprenticeship system rather than from PortSwigger's budget. There are two possible scenarios, and both are simple.

A

If PortSwigger pays the apprenticeship levy

UK payroll above £3m a year

Levy employers already set aside 0.5% of payroll into a training pot, money that is use it or lose it. The £18,000 draws down from that existing pot: funds already earmarked for exactly this purpose.

New training spend: £0
B

If PortSwigger is below the levy threshold

Non-levy employer

Government funds 95% of the training directly. The remaining 5%, at most £900 across the whole programme, is frequently covered in full by a levy transfer from a larger employer.

Typical training spend: £0 to £900
In either scenario the training is effectively already paid for. PortSwigger's real commitment is a genuine wage for genuine work, and that is the part I intend to earn.

Based on the published programme cost and current UK apprenticeship funding rules. I am happy to walk your people team through the mechanics.
